最近围绕特朗普访华、中美关系"软着陆"的讨论，绕不开一个问题：台湾会不会成为交易筹码？

这个问题很重要，但它是错误的起点。

## 台湾不是筹码，是测量工具

筹码是可以被交换的东西。但台湾在中美博弈中的功能，从来不是用来交换——而是用来**测量**。

每当台湾被放上谈判桌，双方其实都在做同一件事：测试对方的底线在哪里，以及对方是否真的有意愿越过那条线。台湾议题被公开讨论，恰恰说明真正的交易还没发生。真正的交易是沉默的。

特朗普在台湾问题上的暧昧，不是出卖，是**战略模糊的商业版本**——他用不确定性换取谈判空间，这一点和历届美国政府的逻辑其实没有本质区别，只是语言更粗粝，包装更少。

## 交易稳定期的真正含义

"交易稳定期"这个说法值得细究。它描述的是一种双方都不想打、但也都不打算让步的状态——用持续的小交易来管理大冲突，用议题切割来避免全面对抗。

这种稳定是真实的，但它的代价被低估了。

交易政治运作的前提，是双方都足够强大，可以直接谈条件。问题在于，**当规则让位于交易，中间那一大批国家的处境就变了**。

新加坡、韩国、日本、越南——这些国家过去几十年的战略空间，很大程度上建立在一套多边规则体系上。这套体系不完美，但它提供了一种保护：大国不能随便吃掉小国，因为有规则在。

一旦中美确立了纯交易模式，规则就降格为谈判的背景噪音。中等国家失去的，不是某个具体的保障，而是**可预期性本身**。

## 知识分子应该问的问题

很多评论在问：特朗普能从中国拿到什么？习近平愿意给多少？

这些是生意人的问题。

学者应该问的是：这一轮交易完成之后，下一次危机到来时，还有没有一套大家都认可的语言可以用？

国际秩序的韧性，不在于大国之间没有冲突，而在于冲突发生时有没有共同的框架来处理。交易政治最大的破坏，不是某个具体的结果，而是它**消耗了框架本身的合法性**。

这才是台湾以外，真正值得盯住的东西。

The debate surrounding Trump's China visit and the so-called US-China "soft landing" keeps circling back to one question: will Taiwan become a bargaining chip?

It's an important question. But it's the wrong starting point.

## Taiwan Is Not a Chip — It's a Gauge

A bargaining chip is something you can exchange. Taiwan's function in US-China competition has never been exchange — it's **measurement**.

Every time Taiwan is placed on the table, both sides are doing the same thing: testing where the other's red line is, and whether they actually intend to cross it. The fact that Taiwan is being discussed openly is precisely the sign that no real deal has been struck. Real deals are silent.

Trump's ambiguity on Taiwan is not a betrayal — it's the commercial version of strategic ambiguity. He trades uncertainty for negotiating room, which is not fundamentally different from every previous administration. The language is coarser, the packaging thinner, but the logic holds.

## What "Transactional Equilibrium" Actually Means

The phrase "transactional equilibrium" deserves scrutiny. It describes a state where neither side wants to fight, but neither intends to concede — managing large conflicts through a series of small deals, slicing issues apart to avoid full confrontation.

This stability is real. But its costs are underestimated.

Transactional politics works on the premise that both parties are powerful enough to set terms directly. The problem is that **when rules give way to deals, the position of everyone in between changes**.

Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Vietnam — these countries built their strategic room over the past few decades largely on a multilateral rules-based system. Imperfect as it was, the system offered a form of protection: major powers couldn't simply absorb smaller ones, because there were rules.

Once the US and China lock into a purely transactional mode, rules are demoted to background noise in negotiations. What middle powers lose is not any specific guarantee — it's **predictability itself**.

## The Question Intellectuals Should Be Asking

Most commentary asks: what can Trump extract from China? How much is Xi willing to give?

These are businessmen's questions.

The question scholars should ask is: once this round of dealing is done, when the next crisis arrives, will there still be a shared language to handle it?

The resilience of international order doesn't lie in the absence of conflict between great powers — it lies in whether there's a common framework for managing conflict when it comes. The deepest damage transactional politics does is not any specific outcome. It's that it **erodes the legitimacy of the framework itself**.

That — not Taiwan — is what deserves our attention.
